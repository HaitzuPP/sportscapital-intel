1. Real Valladolid

La Liga 2  /  Spain

Ownership and Acquisition

Ronaldo Nazario, the two-time Ballon d'Or winner and former Barcelona and Real Madrid striker, acquired a 51% controlling stake in Real Valladolid on 3 September 2018 for approximately €30 million, assuming the role of club president. The purchase was structured through his holding entity Tara Sports 2018, with Ronaldo holding an additional 21.68% directly. Over time he acquired a further 10% from former honorary president Carlos Suárez. By October 2024, when he injected a further €5.1 million into Tara Sports during active sale negotiations, his total shareholding had reached approximately 82.7%. That injection valued the club at just over €100 million, a figure that proved aspirational given subsequent events.

There is no material Saudi co-investor group associated with Real Valladolid. The SMC Group (Saudi Media Company), led by Mohamed Al-Khereiji, is instead linked to UD Almeria, where Cristiano Ronaldo (unrelated) acquired a 25% stake in early 2026. The confusion between the two Ronaldos is common in secondary coverage and should be noted as a due diligence flag.

Sale and Current Ownership

In May 2025, following the club's third relegation in two seasons, Ronaldo sold his majority stake to Grupo Ignite, a Mexican investor group led by Gabriel Solares and Enrique Uruñuela, for approximately €50 million. Ronaldo paid €30 million on entry in 2019 and exits at €50 million after three relegations — a meaningful data point on floor valuations for second-division Spanish clubs with a functional fanbase. The transaction is the cleanest public price discovery in La Liga 2 ownership and should anchor any comparative valuation exercise for peer clubs.

Ben Oldman Partners holds approximately 33% of Grupo Ignite, having converted a prior loan to Ronaldo into equity during the sale process. Ben Oldman is a European fund with offices in Luxembourg, Madrid, London, and Milan. The structure means Valladolid now has a Mexican controlling group (Grupo Ignite) and a European institutional co-investor (Ben Oldman), both with ongoing financial exposure. This is a more sophisticated capital structure than most La Liga 2 clubs carry.

The key operational continuity is Fernando González Velasco (Commercial Director). He holds a LaLiga Business School background and prior experience at Legends Global and ASM Global Europe. His professional process runs counter to the ad-hoc commercial approach typical of family-office club management. He is the most direct commercial contact at the club. Grupo Ignite is not a single-asset buyer; the group's stated appetite is for multi-club structures, which means any commercial conversation that references portfolio-level relevance will land differently than a Valladolid-only pitch.

Commercial Activity

Valladolid's commercial expansion under Ronaldo fell well short of what the name implied. The club's main shirt sponsor throughout much of the Ronaldo era was Estrella Galicia, a regional beer brand, in a deal valued at approximately $2.5 million per year. Total sponsorship revenue for the 2022/23 season was reported at over $7.8 million, spread across a portfolio dominated by local pharmaceuticals, healthcare, and food brands. The club signed a four-year kit deal with Adidas in December 2019. Spanish airline Air Europa came on as a shorts sponsor in January 2020.

Crypto and digital asset activity was minimal. In a October 2023 interview, club director David Espinar explicitly stated that the club had not pursued NFT or cryptocurrency initiatives and had no near-term plans to do so. Ronaldo's personal crypto exposure (his 2022 NFT partnership with Binance) is unrelated to the club.

2. Palermo FC

Serie B  /  Italy

CFG Acquisition

City Football Group (CFG), the Abu Dhabi United Group-owned multi-club platform controlled by Sheikh Mansour bin Zayed Al Nahyan, acquired a majority stake in Palermo FC on 4 July 2022 for approximately €13 million. The announcement was made in the presence of Manchester City CEO Ferran Soriano. CFG took a 71% majority stake, while outgoing owner Dario Mirri retained 20% and remained as club chairman. A further 9% stayed with Rinaldo Sagramola. The acquisition made Palermo CFG's eleventh club globally and its entry point into Italian football. The timing was deliberate: Palermo had just won Serie C playoff promotion over Padova on 12 June 2022, returning to Serie B for the first time since 2019.

The €13 million entry price reflects the club's position at the bottom of the professional pyramid at the time of acquisition, not current asset value. For context, New York City FC, another CFG club, reported over $61 million in annual revenue. CFG's ability to absorb Palermo at low acquisition cost while injecting infrastructure, data, and recruitment expertise is the operational thesis for the investment.

How CFG Operates Palermo

CFG's model at Palermo follows its established playbook: central data and recruitment infrastructure is shared across the network, while the club retains local identity and local management. The club inaugurated a new training center in Torretta in 2024, the first purpose-built facility in the club's history. CFG brought in Eugenio Corini, a former Palermo player and Serie A manager, as head coach for the first Serie B season in 2022-23. The club has also developed a Palermo Museum at the Renzo Barbera stadium and launched the Palermo City Football Academy as part of its grassroots-to-first-team pipeline. Mirri's continued chairmanship maintains Sicilian political and commercial relationships that CFG, as a foreign owner, would not otherwise have access to.

Commercial Activity

Commercial output remains modest relative to Serie A peers. The main shirt sponsor for 2023/24 was SumUp, the London-based fintech payments company, with its branding across the Renzo Barbera and on kit. Kit sponsors that season also included Old Wild West (burgers and steak house chain), Bisaten (paints and varnishes), and A29 (an energy services company). The partner profile suggests CFG has not yet leveraged its global commercial relationships to bring blue-chip sponsors into Palermo. No fan token, crypto, or digital asset partnerships are publicly documented. Before CFG's arrival, the club had a five-year partnership with Infront Sport for commercial and media rights, signed during the Serie C period.

The Renzo Barbera stadium has a capacity of 36,365. The city of Palermo and the club recently signed an agreement extending the tenancy, laying groundwork for a framework lasting up to 99 years, which resolves what had been a medium-term stadium uncertainty for CFG's planning horizon. The Renzo Barbera hosted games at Italia 90 and remains one of the larger operational stadiums in southern Italy.

Palermo has a historically large and loyal fanbase concentrated in the city and western Sicily, with a secondary following among Sicilian emigrants in northern Italy and the diaspora. Attendance figures for Serie B have been solid relative to the division: the Renzo Barbera regularly draws crowds in the mid-to-high teens of thousands for league games, well above the Italian second-tier average.

3. Venezia FC

Serie B  /  Italy

Ownership Structure

Venezia FC is owned by a US-led investor consortium operating through the holding entity VFC Newco 2020. Duncan Niederauer, former CEO of the New York Stock Exchange and former Goldman Sachs banker, took over as president in February 2020 after transitioning from passive to controlling investor. Niederauer built the club's identity around disciplined commercial management and the Venice brand, explicitly positioning Venezia as a global lifestyle club rather than a regional Italian football team.

In July 2024, the club announced a significant capital raise from existing and new investors. New entrants included APEX Capital, Chiron Sports Group, and PSALM Capital. An Operating Committee of 11 members was formed to govern the club collectively. Rob Hamwee (New Mountain Capital) and Jamie Reigle, formerly of Manchester United, the LA Rams, and Formula E, joined the governance structure. The total raise was reported at approximately €40 million, though some sources cite a figure closer to €43.7 million.

The fundraise was catalyzed in part by Drake, the Canadian rapper and producer, who became a confirmed investor in the club and was instrumental in closing the round within two weeks after the club faced a near-term bankruptcy scenario in summer 2024. Without the capital raise, Venezia would have faced salary default and potential relegation to Serie D. Drake's involvement transformed what was a distressed funding event into a global media moment.

In September 2025, the club appointed Romano Cinelli as Head of Commerce, a new standalone role consolidating ticketing, sponsorship and rights, and merchandising under a single commercial function. Cinelli brings 20 years of senior experience at Nike (General Manager Southern Europe Sportswear), Converse EMEA, and The Athlete's Foot EMEA, signaling an explicit upgrade in commercial execution capability.

NOCTA / Drake / Nike Kit Deal

In August 2024, Venezia announced NOCTA, Drake's Nike sub-label, as the club's official technical partner for the 2024/25 season. The deal replaced a three-year partnership with Kappa and represented NOCTA's first appearance in professional football. The home kit debuted on 8 August 2024: a black base with gold detailing and a starry pattern referencing the Venetian night sky. The away kit reinterpreted Venezia's traditional orange and green in a streetwear register. A goalkeeper kit, pre-match range, and third kit (released December 2024 under the "Flow Together" campaign) completed the full collection, presented by SoccerBible as one of the most complete kit collections of the season.

Reception was polarized. The kits were priced at €120 each, making them the most expensive replica kits in European club football, which generated significant criticism around accessibility. Tactically, the cultural crossover worked: coverage spanned Hypebeast, Highsnobiety, and mainstream football outlets simultaneously, a combination few clubs achieve. The club has historically reported that 95% of online kit sales originate outside Italy, a metric that validates the brand-first commercial model.

Fan Base and Brand

Venezia's global following is disproportionate to its sporting record. The club operates with an explicitly international audience strategy: its social media channels are styled closer to a luxury fashion magazine than a football club, and its Instagram presence draws comparisons to MUNDIAL and Vogue. Generation Z engagement is the core target. Venice as a city, combined with the club's commitment to design-led kits since a rebrand beginning around 2021, generates consistent organic virality. Commercial reach is primarily digital: 95% of kit revenue from outside Italy is the most cited signal. The stadium, Stadio Pier Luigi Penzo, seats approximately 11,150-12,000 and is one of the smallest in Serie A when the club is promoted. The club has announced plans for a new €92 million, 18,500-capacity stadium in Tessera (the "Bosco dello Sport" / Sports Forest development), with construction expected to begin in 2025-26.

Risk note: Two Serie A seasons in four years, both ending in relegation. The yo-yo pattern reflects a squad budget that is competitive in Serie B but thin in Serie A. The brand engine is legitimate and commercially differentiated, but revenue from kits and digital channels alone does not fund a Serie A-competitive wage bill. The €40 million capital raise in 2024 stabilized a near-bankruptcy situation; the structural question is whether the commercial model can grow fast enough to support consistent top-flight football. The new stadium development, if it proceeds on schedule, would transform matchday economics, but construction risk and the club's current Serie B status add uncertainty to that timeline.
