Key trends

  • US investors control 11 of 20 Premier League clubs in 2025-26. Broadcast revenue runs at ~£3.5bn per season, 3.5x the Bundesliga and 5x Ligue 1. The revenue gap compounds ownership premiums: clubs are bought on media rights trajectory, not current income.
  • Saudi PIF's Newcastle takeover (Oct 2021, £305m for 80%) has produced a ~3x gain in four years; the club is now valued at approximately $1.3bn. PIF injected a further £127m in 2023 and £97m in 2024 to fund squad investment. It is the cleanest proof of concept for state capital in English football.
  • Everton's Friedkin acquisition (Dec 2024) converted £450.75m of Farhad Moshiri's shareholder loans into equity, with total consideration exceeding £400m. The Friedkin Group already owns AS Roma, making it one of two confirmed cross-market MCO structures in the Premier League alongside Crystal Palace and Eagle Football. A reported Roma-Everton player swap arrangement has since drawn Premier League regulatory scrutiny on conflicts of interest.
  • The MCO structure now covers more than a third of Premier League clubs. Eagle Football (Textor) connects Crystal Palace to Lyon and RWD Molenbeek. BlueCo (Boehly) connects Chelsea to Strasbourg and Vitesse. Silver Lake connects Man City (via CFG) to IMG and the Endeavor media empire. The Premier League's conflict-of-interest rules were designed for individual club ownership, not layered holding structures.
  • 49ers Enterprises took majority control of Leeds United in 2023 following promotion, applying the NFL franchise operator cross-ownership template the group has used at eight clubs globally. The model: buy at lower-division prices, use promotion as the valuation event, use NFL network effects to generate commercial revenue above what the playing squad would normally produce.
  • Only two clubs remain under British private majority ownership: Brentford (Matthew Benham, Smartodds founder, controlling shareholder since 2012) and Brighton (Tony Bloom, professional gambler, since 2009). Both built data infrastructure before foreign capital recognised the asset class. Both have over-performed against squad spend relative to expectation.

Key trends

  • Three clubs rejected the 2022 CVC deal: Real Madrid, Barcelona, and Athletic Club. The deal gave CVC $2.2bn for 8.5-11% of broadcasting revenues across a 50-year term. Clubs traded half a century of commercial upside for immediate liquidity. Several accepting clubs have since acknowledged the terms were disadvantageous; Barcelona and Real Madrid retained full upside.
  • Valencia's financial position is the starkest ownership failure in La Liga. Peter Lim reportedly sought €400m for a sale in early 2025 while the club carried €340m in debt. The Nou Mestalla stadium has been under construction since 2007 with no completion date. Lim has cycled through 11 coaches since 2014. The absentee foreign owner with no football infrastructure is the model the asset class has learned to avoid.
  • Girona's City Football Group ownership (majority stake since 2017) became European football's clearest MCO regulatory test when both Girona and Manchester City qualified for the 2023-24 Champions League simultaneously. UEFA required player segregation and blocked transfers between affiliated clubs. The outcome validated UEFA's multi-club rules but also demonstrated that the rules add friction without resolving the fundamental competitive conflict.
  • The four member-owned clubs (Real Madrid, Barcelona, Athletic Bilbao, Osasuna) retain the 1992 socios exemption, which required sound finances at the time of conversion. Real Madrid generated €831m in 2023-24 revenue, the highest of any European club that season, under full member ownership. The exemption has not constrained commercial performance at the clubs with strong governance.
  • US ownership is entering through mid-tier and recently promoted clubs: Real Oviedo (Asturias Football Group, US investors since 2013, including former NFL quarterback John Elway as a minority backer), Mallorca (Andy Kohlberg US group, 2016), Elche (Argentine agent Christian Bragarnik, 2020). The template mirrors the NFL operator cross-ownership model: acquire at second-division prices, use promotion as the value event.
  • Girona's rise from third division to Champions League in six seasons under CFG ownership is the fastest commercially engineered ascent in modern La Liga history. CFG used the Girona platform to develop players from its global network before qualifying for European competition. The model will face a structural ceiling if UEFA extends multi-club conflict rules further.
Bundesliga
Germany · 18 clubs · 2025-26

Owner type

Member / fan-owned
Corporate (50+1 exception)

15 of 18 clubs are member-owned. The three corporate exceptions (Bayer AG, VW, Red Bull) predate the 50+1 rule and cannot be replicated under current DFL rules.

Key trends

  • The 50+1 rule requires members to hold majority voting rights at all DFL clubs. The revised 2023 DFL statutes explicitly state no further exceptions will be granted. 15 of 18 Bundesliga clubs are fully member-controlled. The three corporate-exception clubs (Bayer AG, Volkswagen, Red Bull) predate 1998 DFL rules and represent closed legacy positions.
  • Red Bull Leipzig's compliance with 50+1 is technical, not substantive. The club has five registered members, all Red Bull employees. Voting rights are functionally held by Red Bull. The DFL has determined this satisfies the rule on a strict reading. German fan groups have campaigned against the structure since Leipzig's founding in 2009. No other club has successfully replicated it since the DFL identified and closed the approach.
  • Hoffenheim returned to full 50+1 compliance in November 2024 after Dietmar Hopp (SAP co-founder) transferred majority voting rights back to the registered members. Hopp had effectively controlled the club since 1989, investing an estimated €350m over three decades. The transfer was voluntary but followed sustained fan protests and DFL pressure. It closes the last active personal-exception holding in German football.
  • Hamburger SV's return to the Bundesliga (2025-26) after a seven-year absence is structurally significant: HSV, with 130,000 members, is the largest club in Germany by registered membership and resisted multiple investor approaches during three years in the second division. The club's survival through relegation without an ownership change validates 50+1 under financial stress at a scale no previous case had tested.
  • The Bundesliga's broadcast revenue gap versus the Premier League (~£1bn/season domestic vs ~£3.5bn) is partly a consequence of the same ownership model that protects German football culture. The 50+1 structure has historically limited the ability to negotiate centralised commercial deals of the type La Liga struck with CVC. German clubs have accepted this trade-off explicitly: the 2023 DFL member vote on private equity investment failed to reach the required two-thirds majority.

Ligue 1 has the highest MCO penetration in European top-division football. 9 of 18 clubs share at least one shareholder with another club.

Key trends

  • The LFP launched its own streaming platform, Ligue 1+, in August 2025 at €14.99/month after severing ties with DAZN. DAZN had exited after one year of a four-year deal, citing the LFP's failure to address broadcast piracy. Ligue 1+ reached 600,000 subscribers in its first week, with the LFP targeting one million by season end. DAZN subsequently re-entered as a secondary distribution partner.
  • Paris FC's promotion in 2025 under Arnault family (LVMH) ownership produced the first Paris derby since 1997. PSG won the opening fixture 2-1. Arnault's stated intent is a 10-year institution-building programme funded by LVMH commercial infrastructure, not short-term squad investment. It is the first credible long-term challenge to PSG's domestic monopoly in the modern era.
  • US PE penetration is deeper in Ligue 1 than any other European top division: RedBird Capital (Toulouse, 2020), Eagle Football/John Textor (Lyon, 2022), BlueCo/Todd Boehly (Strasbourg, 2023), Frank McCourt (Marseille, 2016). Lyon was placed under DNCG financial supervision in 2024 after recurring losses under Textor's management. US PE capability in European club operations has not yet matched US PE deal execution speed.
  • Nine of 18 Ligue 1 clubs share at least one shareholder with another professional club, the highest MCO concentration in any European top division. Player loan networks between affiliated clubs are common, creating competitive integrity concerns the LFP has acknowledged but not structurally resolved.
  • Monaco's tax residency in the Principality creates a structural salary advantage: players pay Monaco income tax rates rather than French rates, a significant differential at top-of-scale wages. The LFP has made repeated attempts to apply French financial rules to Monaco fully; each has been partially blocked on jurisdictional grounds. It is the one structural competitive advantage in Ligue 1 that other clubs cannot replicate regardless of ownership.

Owner type

Member / foundation
Dutch private owner
Asian investor

The Eredivisie remains primarily member- and foundation-owned. Foreign capital is entering through smaller clubs rather than the traditional Big Three.

Key trends

  • Vitesse Arnhem had its professional licence permanently revoked by the KNVB in July 2025 after a civil court upheld the decision. The KNVB cited "a multi-year pattern of deception, circumvention, and undermining of the licensing system" following an ownership dispute with US investor Coley Parry. The club is expected to dissolve after 133 years. The 2025-26 Eredivisie began with 19 clubs: no eligible replacement could be identified in time.
  • The Vitesse case is the most instructive foreign ownership failure in Dutch football. Parry, a US private equity investor, took control in 2022, then failed to meet financial integrity requirements set by the KNVB. The KNVB's decision to run a 19-club season rather than promote an ineligible replacement is the correct governance call, but it sets a difficult precedent: the league structure is now distorted for an entire season as a direct consequence of a single foreign ownership failure.
  • Ajax's governance crisis (2023-24) resulted in the dismissal of multiple CEOs and sporting directors within 18 months, a third-place Eredivisie finish, and a significant decline in squad value. The supervisory board structure, which separates strategic oversight from operational management, was identified as the primary cause of decision-making paralysis. Ajax's supervisory board has since been restructured, but institutional trust has not fully recovered.
  • Asian capital is entering the Eredivisie through mid-table clubs rather than the established Big Three (Ajax, PSV, Feyenoord). Yanmar (Japanese industrial group) acquired Almere City FC in 2022; South Korean investor Choi Soon-Ho has controlled Fortuna Sittard since 2018. Both acquisitions are modest in scale, using Dutch football as a European operational platform rather than a brand investment.
  • The Eredivisie's broadcast rights generate approximately €70m per season, compared to £3.5bn for the Premier League. The resulting valuation gap means Eredivisie clubs trade at significant discounts to equivalent-size English clubs, making the Netherlands the most accessible acquisition market in the Big Five adjacent leagues for investors willing to accept lower liquidity and smaller media exposure.
