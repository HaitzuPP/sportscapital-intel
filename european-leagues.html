Key trends

  • US investors now control 11 of 20 Premier League clubs. The league has become the primary target for North American sports capital seeking media rights compounding: broadcast revenue runs at ~£3.5bn per season, dwarfing every other European top division.
  • Saudi PIF's Newcastle acquisition (2021, £305m for 80%) was the first direct sovereign wealth entry into the PL. PIF's subsequent reported alliance with Abu Dhabi United Group (Man City) on a £41bn media infrastructure deal signals coordinated Gulf state positioning across sport and broadcast.
  • Everton's Friedkin acquisition (2024, ~£400m) followed the collapse of 777 Partners' bid, marking the first distressed ownership transfer in the modern PL era. Friedkin already owns AS Roma, creating a cross-market portfolio.
  • 49ers Enterprises' Leeds model demonstrates NFL franchise operators using European football as a growth asset: the 49ers took majority control in 2023 following promotion, using the same cross-ownership template applied at eight other clubs globally.
  • Only two clubs remain under majority British private ownership: Brentford (Matthew Benham, Smartodds founder) and Brighton (Tony Bloom, professional gambler). Both built on data infrastructure before foreign capital recognised the asset class.

Key trends

  • Four clubs retain the 1992 socios exemption (Real Madrid, Barcelona, Athletic Bilbao, Osasuna). The exemption was granted to clubs that demonstrated sound finances; all four have since become among the most financially stable in Europe, validating the model but also limiting outside capital access.
  • CVC's 2022 deal with La Liga injected capital at league level (10% stake in the commercial vehicle for approximately €2.7bn), not individual clubs. This is structurally different from PE buyouts: clubs received cash upfront but ceded future commercial upside, a trade several clubs now regret.
  • Girona's City Football Group ownership created a competitive conflict when Girona qualified for the Champions League in 2023-24 while Man City (also CFG) competed in the same tournament. UEFA's multi-club rules required player segregation and transfer restrictions. The Girona case is the clearest stress test of MCO limits yet seen in European football.
  • US-linked ownership is accelerating in the lower reaches: Real Oviedo (US group since 2013), Mallorca (Andy Kohlberg since 2016), Elche (Bragarnik, Argentine agent network). The pattern mirrors the NFL franchise operator playbook: buy a mid-table club, use promotion as the value event.
  • Valencia's Peter Lim era (2014 to present) is the most visible cautionary tale in La Liga ownership. The club has cycled through 11 coaches since 2014, posted consistent losses, and faces a stadium redevelopment stalemate. Fan protests have been sustained for three years. Lim is the archetype of the absentee foreign owner with no operational football infrastructure.

Owner type

Member / fan-owned
Corporate (50+1 exception)

15 of 18 clubs are member-owned. The three corporate exceptions (Bayer AG, VW, Red Bull) predate the 50+1 rule and cannot be replicated under current DFL rules.

Key trends

  • The 50+1 rule requires members to hold majority voting rights at all DFL clubs. The revised 2023 DFL statutes explicitly state no further exceptions will be granted, closing the route used by Bayer Leverkusen (Bayer AG), Wolfsburg (Volkswagen) and Leipzig (Red Bull).
  • Hoffenheim's return to 50+1 in November 2024 was the most significant structural ownership change in the Bundesliga in years. Dietmar Hopp (SAP co-founder) transferred his majority voting stake back to the club's registered members, ending 20 years of de facto corporate control. The DFL framed it as voluntary; fan groups had campaigned against Hopp's control since 2019.
  • Hamburger SV's return to the Bundesliga (2025-26) after a seven-year absence is notable precisely because the club was never sold during relegation. With 130,000 members, HSV is the largest club in Germany by membership and resisted multiple investor approaches. Its survival validates the 50+1 model under financial stress.
  • Red Bull Leipzig remains the most contested entity in German football. Red Bull controls the club via a nominal membership structure with five members, all Red Bull employees. The DFL has determined this complies with 50+1 on a technicality. No other club has replicated the structure since the DFL closed the loophole.
  • Bundesliga TV rights (~£1bn per season) lag the Premier League by a factor of 3.5x. The ownership model that protects German clubs from speculative capital also constrains revenue-sharing deals of the type La Liga struck with CVC. The trade-off is structural: fan ownership preserves culture, limits commercial upside.

Ligue 1 has the highest MCO penetration in European top-division football. 9 of 18 clubs share at least one shareholder with another club.

Key trends

  • Paris FC's promotion to Ligue 1 in 2025 under Arnault family ownership (LVMH's Bernard Arnault) creates the first Paris derby since 1997. Arnault's stated intent is to build a genuine rival to PSG over 10 years, funded by LVMH infrastructure and Parisian corporate sponsorship. PSG's Nasser Al-Khelaifi has publicly expressed concern about the competitive and commercial threat.
  • US PE has penetrated Ligue 1 more deeply than any other European top division: RedBird Capital (Toulouse, 2020), Eagle Football / Textor (Lyon, 2022), BlueCo / Boehly (Strasbourg, 2023), McCourt (Marseille, 2016). All four are financially complex and operationally high-risk. Lyon was placed under DNCG financial supervision in 2024 after posting recurring losses under Textor.
  • MCO creates structural distortions in France: 9 of 18 Ligue 1 clubs share at least one shareholder with another football club. Player loan networks between affiliated clubs are common, raising competitive integrity questions the LFP has not fully resolved.
  • The DAZN broadcast rights collapse (2024) exposed the structural weakness of French football media rights. DAZN won the rights then defaulted on payment obligations, triggering a legal dispute and short-term broadcast chaos. Canal+ stepped in as emergency broadcaster. Ligue 1 rights are valued at roughly one-fifth of the Premier League on a per-club basis.
  • Monaco's Rybolovlev ownership (2011) was the first major foreign sovereign-adjacent acquisition in Ligue 1. Monaco's tax residency in the Principality creates a structural competitive advantage on player wages that other clubs cannot replicate. The LFP's repeated attempts to apply French financial rules to Monaco have been only partially successful.

Owner type

Member / foundation
Dutch private owner

The Eredivisie remains primarily member- and foundation-owned. Foreign capital is entering through smaller clubs rather than the traditional Big Three.

Key trends

  • Vitesse Arnhem lost its professional licence in July 2025 following a prolonged ownership dispute with US investor Coley Parry. Vitesse was found to have breached financial integrity rules after Parry failed to meet capital requirements. The club was demoted and replaced in the Eredivisie by the first-division playoff winner. It is the first top-division licence revocation in Dutch football.
  • Asian capital is entering the Eredivisie through mid-table clubs rather than the traditional Big Three. Yanmar (Japanese industrial group) acquired Almere City in 2022; South Korean investor Choi Soon-Ho has controlled Fortuna Sittard since 2018. Both acquisitions are modest in scale, using Dutch football as a European foothold rather than a brand play.
  • Ajax's governance failure (2023-24) is the most studied structural crisis in Dutch football. The club sacked multiple CEOs and sporting directors within 18 months, finished third in the Eredivisie, and saw its squad value decline sharply. The supervisory board model, which separates the management board from strategic oversight, was widely criticised for creating decision-making paralysis.
  • Eredivisie TV rights generate approximately 70m euros per season, compared to 3.5bn pounds for the Premier League. The valuation gap means Eredivisie clubs trade at significant discounts to equivalent-size English clubs, making them attractive entry points for investors using Dutch football as an MCO feeder hub rather than a primary asset.
  • The Big Three (Ajax, PSV, Feyenoord) have dominated the Eredivisie title for all but five of the last 60 seasons. PSV won the 2023-24 title, their 25th. The structural concentration at the top is unlikely to change: the three clubs' combined commercial revenue exceeds the remaining 15 clubs combined, compounding over time.
